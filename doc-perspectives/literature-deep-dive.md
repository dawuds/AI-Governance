# AI Governance: A Strategic Literature Synthesis

*Mapping the academic and policy battlegrounds of the 21st-century AI stack.*

---

## 🏛️ The Pro-Governance Case: "Responsible Innovation"

The foundational literature for Track 1 (Compliance-as-Code) argues that governance is the mandatory prerequisite for mass enterprise adoption and public trust.

### 1. The Trust-Compliance Link (McKinsey/OECD, 2024-2025)
- **Core Argument:** Institutional trust is the primary "fuel" for AI deployment. 
- **Literature Finding:** 2025 surveys (e.g., Brookings/Purdue) show that 76% of organizations have AI policies, but public skepticism is at an all-time high. Robust, binding frameworks like the **EU AI Act** are seen as the "Social License to Operate" that allows companies to scale without public backlash.
- **Reference:** *OECD AI Principles Report (2024)*; *CIGI Digital Policy Hub (2025)*.

### 2. Standardization as a Market Opener (Schomberg, 2024)
- **Core Argument:** Standardized frameworks (ISO 42001, NIST) act as "Market Interoperability" tools.
- **Literature Finding:** Just as ISO 9001 revolutionized manufacturing quality, **ISO/IEC 42001** is viewed in the literature as a tool to reduce "Transaction Costs" in global AI trade. It allows a developer in Malaysia to prove "Duty of Care" to a customer in the EU using a shared technical language.
- **Reference:** *CEN/CENELEC Standardization Roadmap (2024)*.

## ⚖️ The Critical Case: "Governance as a Double-Edged Sword"

The literature for Track 2 (Strategic Critique) examines the unintended systemic consequences of top-down AI regulation.

### 1. Governance Theater & The "Analogy Trap" (Selbst et al., 2026)
- **Core Argument:** Current AI governance often suffers from "Theater"—documentation that provides the *appearance* of oversight without constraining model behavior.
- **Literature Finding:** *The Digital Gorilla (arXiv, 2026)* diagnoses this as the "Analogy Trap"—treating AI as a simple tool rather than a dynamic societal actor. Critics argue that "Checkbox Compliance" (represented by static JSONs) fails to address **Agentic AI** behaviors.
- **Reference:** *Selbst & Green, "Algorithmic Accountability Theater" (2025)*.

### 2. Regulatory Capture & The "Innovation Moat" (Corporate Europe Observatory, 2024)
- **Core Argument:** Complex regulations act as barriers to entry that favor trillion-dollar incumbents.
- **Literature Finding:** 2024 studies on the **EU AI Act** document how "General-Purpose AI" rules were influenced by intense lobbying from Silicon Valley and European "national champions." The high cost of compliance (Layer 3-5 in our architecture) effectively "Illegalizes the Competition" from resource-constrained startups.
- **Reference:** *Cambridge Handbook of the Law, Ethics and Policy of AI (2024)*.

### 3. Epistemic Colonialism (Birhane, 2020 / Mohamed et al., 2020)
- **Core Argument:** "Universal" AI ethics (UNESCO/OECD) often mask Western-centric hierarchies.
- **Literature Finding:** Leading scholars like **Abeba Birhane** argue that the uncritical export of Western AI governance to the Global South constitutes **"Epistemicide"**—the erasure of local knowledge systems.
- **Reference:** *Birhane, "Algorithmic Colonization of Africa" (2020)*; *Mohamed, Png, & Isaac, "Decolonial AI" (2020)*.

## 🧬 Synthesis: From "Passive" to "Active" Governance

The most recent literature (2025-2026) suggests a shift away from "Evidence Snapshots" toward **Runtime Enforcement**.

- **Proof-Carrying Decisions (PCD):** A technical method to ensure every AI action has a verifiable, human-traceable causal path.
- **Active Governance Envelopes:** Moving beyond `checklist.json` to real-time telemetry and circuit-breakers that operate *inside* the model's inference loop.
